A History Shaped by Sea and Time

For over a thousand years, San Juan de Gaztelugatxe has stood as a place of faith, defense, and legend along the Basque coast.

Scroll Down

1334

The Siege of Gaztelugatxe

Seven Biscayan knights barricaded themselves inside the hermitage to resist the forces of King Alfonso XI of Castile. Holding out for over a month on a clifftop accessible only by a narrow bridge, they forced the king's army to withdraw — one of the most celebrated acts of Basque defiance.

1596

Drake's Raid

English privateer Sir Francis Drake attacked the island, looting the hermitage and killing the hermit who lived there. The assault was one of several pirate raids the island endured over the centuries, each time leaving the community to rebuild what the sea and its enemies had taken.

1886

Demolition & Reconstruction

After centuries of raids, fires, and decay, the deteriorated church was fully demolished and rebuilt from the ground up. During demolition, all artefacts found in the earth — coins, cannonballs, fragments of the island's long history — were discarded into the sea, a loss mourned to this day.

1978

Fire & Rebirth

A devastating fire destroyed the hermitage almost entirely. The Basque community and local authorities committed to a full restoration, and the rebuilt structure was inaugurated on 24 June 1980 — Saint John the Baptist's feast day — preserving the symbolic heart of the site.

2017

Dragonstone

HBO's Game of Thrones chose San Juan de Gaztelugatxe as the exterior location for Dragonstone, the ancestral fortress of House Targaryen, in Season 7. The broadcast sent visitor numbers soaring — 75,000 tourists climbed the 241 steps in July 2017 alone, forever changing the island's global profile.
